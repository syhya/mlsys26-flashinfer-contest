**3. Strategic Analysis (The "So What?"):**
- **Root Cause:** Both plan and execution share blame, but the plan is the dominant cause. The plan's analytical bound on BF16 workspace error was wrong: per-partition `O_p` is *not* renormalized to ≤1; it is the unnormalized online-softmax accumulator scaled by `exp(z - m_p)`, whose magnitude per partition can be on the order of `l_p · max|v|`. Quantizing those FP32 partials to BF16 introduces ~0.4–0.8% relative error per element, and after weighted merge across 64 partitions plus a small `l_global` divisor in low-mass partitions, max relative error blew up by ~3 orders of magnitude. The harness `correctness=1.0` flag is misleading because it appears to be a coarse pass/fail under absolute tolerance only; the relative-error explosion still produced a real benchmark-score regression. Execution faithfully implemented the plan, so this is fundamentally a plan-quality failure.
- **Key Insight:** Workspace precision is not a free bandwidth lever in split-K online softmax — partial `O_p` values lack the bounded magnitude that final normalized outputs have, and BF16 storage of unnormalized FP32 sufficient statistics is unsafe. The salvageable concept is *not* "BF16 workspace" but rather the more disciplined idea that the reducer is a high-leverage HBM surface; a safer variant would be reducing partition count (S=32) or compressing only fully-normalized intermediates, not raw accumulators.
- **Identified Risk:** The biggest revealed trap is "fusion-of-wins" overconfidence: the plan correctly fused two validated sibling improvements (validity flag + reducer simplification) but bundled them with one unvalidated, mathematically unsound change. Bundling makes attribution impossible and lets a single bad idea sink two good ones. Additionally, the lineage now has clear evidence that benchmark-relative scoring (variable reference baselines per run) can mask or amplify true kernel-level effects — kernel latency here is comparable to parent, but the score moved sharply.

**4. Actionable Guidance (The "What's Next?"):**
- **Recommend Stripping:** Immediately discard the BF16-`O_tmp` idea from the active gene pool. The unnormalized partial accumulators must remain FP32. Future plans should explicitly forbid quantizing pre-normalization sufficient statistics.
- **Recommend Fusion:** Re-issue a child that fuses *only* the two already-validated wins — sibling 94736692's leaner reducer + parent `bad043e5`'s `tile_has_valid` flag — on top of an FP32 `O_tmp`. This isolates the proven gains and should re-establish a score in the 12.6+ range as the new exploitation baseline.
- **Recommend Exploration:** If reducer HBM traffic is genuinely the bottleneck, explore *partition count reduction* (S=32 or S=16) instead of precision reduction. Halving S linearly halves both write and read bandwidth of `O_tmp` while keeping FP32 exactness; the only cost is less split-K parallelism, which is acceptable in the small-T regime where occupancy is already saturated by the per-partition grid.
- **Recommend Exploration:** Alternatively, fuse the reducer into the compute kernel via a single-block-per-(t,h) cross-warp merge using shared memory, eliminating the `O_tmp` HBM round-trip entirely. This is the principled way to attack the bandwidth target the failed plan was reaching for.
- **Warn:** Do not bundle unvalidated structural changes with already-validated micro-optimizations in a single child. Force one-axis-at-a-time A/B isolation when the change touches numerical precision boundaries.
- **Warn:** Treat `correctness=1.0` from the harness as necessary but not sufficient — `max_relative_error` in the hundreds is a red flag even when the pass gate triggers, and may correlate with downstream score regressions through the benchmark's relative-error component.
