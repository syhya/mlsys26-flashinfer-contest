**3. Strategic Analysis (The "So What?"):**
- **Root Cause:** The outcome is mostly a plan-quality failure, with execution being faithful and correct. The plan reasoned that S=32 would "halve HBM traffic" and improve latency, but in the dominant T=1 workload the split-K grid is `(S, T)`, so reducing S directly reduces the number of resident blocks from 64 to 32 — cutting SM occupancy roughly in half exactly where the kernel is grid-occupancy-bound. Each compute block also doubled its serial work (two 32-slot sub-tiles) without any reduction in shared-memory staging cost. The math was preserved (online-softmax associativity), but the performance model was inverted: bandwidth was not the bottleneck; small-grid occupancy was.
- **Key Insight:** In the small-T regime, the scoring signal is dominated by how well the launch saturates the GPU, not by workspace bandwidth. S=64 is not a wasteful choice — it is load-bearing. Any "halve the workspace" idea (this child) or "quantize the workspace" idea (sibling 1a073d5e) regresses because both attack a non-bottleneck while damaging a real one (occupancy, or numerical fidelity, respectively). The salvageable insight is the opposite of the plan's premise: explore *increasing* parallelism granularity (e.g., per-head split, or per-(head, partition) blocks) rather than coarsening it.
- **Identified Risk:** Two parents-worth of "exploit the iter20 winner" plans have now produced two regressions, each from a different unvalidated structural tweak bundled on top of the validated reducer simplification. The lineage risks systematically misattributing the iter20 win and burning iterations on small-path "optimizations" that worsen it. There is also a measurement-volatility risk: reference baseline swung from 3.03 ms (parent run) to 2.49 ms (this run), a 18% shift that materially compresses the speedup ratio independent of kernel quality.
